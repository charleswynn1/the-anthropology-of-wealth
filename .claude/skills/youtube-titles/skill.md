# YouTube Title Creation

## Purpose

Create compelling YouTube titles that maximize click-through rate (CTR) and engagement. Your title is doing more heavy lifting than you realize — a single title change can be enough to blow up a video. Treat the title as one of the most critical elements of every video, not an afterthought.

## Core Principle: Open Loops

Your title must generate **curiosity** by opening a loop — an unfinished thought that the viewer's brain is driven to close by clicking. If people aren't clicking, the title doesn't open a strong enough loop.

**How to open loops:**
- Make the viewer ask questions: "Why?", "How?", "What happened?"
- Withhold the key piece of information that the video delivers
- Imply a surprising or counterintuitive outcome without revealing it
- Use specificity to make abstract claims feel concrete and real

See @Open Loops.pdf for 16 proven open loop formats with examples.

## Data-Backed Title Checklist

Six evidence-based rules drawn from VidIQ's 9M-title study and Marcus Jones' analysis of 94K titles from the top 100 YouTubers. Run every title through this checklist before finalizing.

1. **Title Length** — **6-8 words / ~42 characters** is the sweet spot. Nearly 60% of top-100 creator titles land here; fewer than 20% of all YouTube titles do. Frontload the hook in the first half.
2. **Point of View** — Default to **neutral** (no personal reference). Use first person when personal experience is the hook. Avoid second person (reads like commands) and third person (reads like headlines about strangers). Top-100 split: 70% neutral, 27% first person, 3% second+third.
3. **Voice** — Default to **neutral** (descriptive phrase, no clear subject). Use active voice when there's a clear action/transformation. Skip passive entirely. Top-100 split: 57% neutral, 42% active, ~1% passive.
4. **Capitalization** — Use **Title Case** (57% of top-100 titles) or **Mixed Case** for emphasis on one key word (28%). Avoid sentence case, all caps, and all lowercase.
5. **Question Marks** — Use when the title naturally creates a curiosity loop ("Wait, what's the answer?"). Don't force statements into questions. VidIQ data showed higher engagement on question titles, but top-100 creators don't overuse them.
6. **Word Choice** — Favor **grounded power words** (unexpected, hidden, overlooked, simple, surprising, exceptional) over **sensational words** (insane, shocking, unbelievable, mind-blowing). Sensational words consistently underperformed in VidIQ's data.

## The 5 Title Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Not Giving People a Reason to Click
Bad: "I Tried Filipino Food"
Good: "I Flew 7,292 Miles to Eat Here"
The second title opens questions — why fly so far? What kind of food? Was it worth it?

### 2. Not Using Proven Formats
Don't invent from scratch. Model titles from outlier videos (videos that massively outperformed a channel's average). Break successful titles into their core structure and adapt:
- "I [bold action] for [time frame] — This Is What Happened"
- "I Woke Up at 4:00 AM Every Day for a Month — This Is What Happened"
- Extract the format, apply your content.

### 3. Using Boring Words
One word can be the difference between clicks and being ignored.
Bad: "How to Edit and Film YouTube Videos"
Good: "How to Edit and Film Cinematic Videos Like a Pro"

Use **power words** — specific words that make titles more intriguing without changing meaning. See @Power Words.pdf for the full reference.

**Four categories of power words:**

| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| **Emotional** | Regret, Life-Changing, Terrifying, Worst, Broken, Ruin/Ruining, Cozy |
| **Authority** | Expert, Pro, Ultimate, Proven, Best, #1, Genius, "As a(n) __" |
| **Urgency** | Now, Instantly, Stop, Wait, "Before You __", "Don't __ Until __", "Before It's Too Late" |
| **Exclusivity** | Secret, Hidden, Actually, Underrated, "No One Knows", "The Truth About __", "What Nobody Tells You" |
| **Grounded** | Unexpected, Hidden, Overlooked, Simple, Surprising, Exceptional — create curiosity without overpromising |
| **⚠ Sensational (use sparingly)** | Insane, Shocking, Unbelievable, Mind-Blowing — consistently underperformed in large-scale data |

**Niche-specific power words matter.** Every niche has words that drive clicks disproportionately. For history/documentary: "terrifying", "deadliest", "secret", "hidden", "what really happened", "the truth about", "nobody talks about".

### 4. Being Too Wordy
Your title competes with dozens of others on screen. If it takes too long to process, viewers' eyes skip to a faster title.

**Rules:**
- Aim for **6-8 words / ~42 characters** — this is the sweet spot where nearly 60% of top-100 creator titles land
- **55 characters** is the outer limit, not the target
- Frontload the most important words — the core message must be clear from the **first half**
- People skim, they don't read word-for-word
- YouTube truncates with "..." on some devices — put the hook first, context second

### 5. Sticking With a Title That's Not Working
Be willing to change titles post-upload. Big creators do this constantly.

**When to change:**
- CTR AND impressions both below average in the first 24 hours → change title or thumbnail
- If CTR and impressions are average, wait until your typical traction window (could be 2-10 days)
- Title changes can take up to a week to show results — be patient

## Title Creation Process

When creating a title for any video:

1. **Identify the most shocking/compelling specific detail** — not the topic in general, but the one fact, number, or moment that would make someone stop scrolling
2. **Choose an open loop format** that fits the content (from the 16 above)
3. **Apply power words** from the appropriate category
4. **Frontload the hook** — the first 5-7 words must convey the core intrigue
5. **Check length** — aim for 6-8 words / ~42 characters. Under 55 characters max. Never bury the hook after character 30. Run through the 6-item checklist (point of view, voice, capitalization, question marks, word choice).
6. **Generate 3-5 variations** using different formats and angles
7. **Pick the one that opens the strongest loop** — which title makes YOU ask the most questions?

## Channel-Specific Guidance

### 60 Seconds of Chaos (Shorts)
- Pattern: `[Specific dramatic hook] | [Event Name]`
- Hook must reference the story's most shocking specific detail
- Examples from channel: "They Marched Soldiers Into a Mushroom Cloud — On Purpose", "A Dropped Wrench Almost Caused a Nuclear Explosion on U.S. Soil"
- Check `shortform/youtube_titles.md` for existing titles
- **Niche exception:** History/documentary titles may run 8-12 words when using proven formats (dramatic hook + event identifier). The 6-8 word guideline is a general benchmark — prioritize clarity and open loops over strict word count.

### 60 Seconds of Chaos — Deep Dives (Longform)
- Pattern: `[Dramatic narrative hook] — [Event, Date]`
- More documentary-feeling — evocative, not clickbait
- Reference the story's most compelling human element or shocking detail
- Examples: "The Night America Almost Nuked Itself — Goldsboro, 1961", "The One Man Who Could Have Ended the World — Cuban Missile Crisis, 1962"
- Check `longform/youtube_titles.md` for existing titles
- **Niche exception:** History/documentary titles may run 8-12 words when using proven formats (dramatic hook + event identifier). The 6-8 word guideline is a general benchmark — prioritize clarity and open loops over strict word count.

### General Rules (All Channels)
- Under 100 characters total
- Check the relevant `youtube_titles.md` for duplicates before finalizing
- Never use generic descriptions as titles ("History of Event X")
- Always include the most specific, surprising detail from the content
- Numbers, dates, and measurements create specificity ("173 Dead", "600 Feet Below", "15 Kilotons")
- All temperatures in Fahrenheit — never Celsius (e.g., "115°F" not "46°C")

## Post-Upload Title Monitoring

After uploading, monitor performance:
- **First 24 hours**: If CTR + impressions are both below channel average, consider a title change
- **Traction window**: Each channel has a typical period before videos gain momentum — don't panic-change during this window
- **Test one variable at a time**: Change title OR thumbnail, not both simultaneously
- **Document what works**: Track which title formats drive the highest CTR in `youtube_titles.md`

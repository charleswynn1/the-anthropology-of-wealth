# YouTube Thumbnail Design

Design YouTube thumbnails that get clicked by applying five psychological constants to every design decision. Use this skill whenever creating [THUMBNAIL] blocks for script.md, writing thumbnail scene descriptions, selecting thumbnail text, or evaluating whether a thumbnail concept will perform. Load this skill before W3d (thumbnail generation) in the pipeline.

## The Only Job a Thumbnail Has

A thumbnail makes a promise the viewer cannot walk away from. That is the entire job. Most thumbnails fail because they try to **describe** the video instead of **promising** something the viewer needs to resolve. A thumbnail of a man standing in a bank vault describes. A thumbnail of a man discovering something alarming inside a bank vault promises.

The difference between a described scene and a promised scene is the information gap. Description closes the gap — the viewer already knows what the video is about. Promise opens the gap — the viewer knows *something* is happening but needs to click to find out what.

Before any thumbnail moves to generation, it must pass this test:

> **"If a viewer saw only this thumbnail with no title, would they feel compelled to click — or would they feel like they already got the point?"**
>
> If the answer is "already got the point," the thumbnail describes. Redesign it around a promise.

## The Five Psychological Constants

These five elements have driven click-through rates since before YouTube existed — movie posters, VHS covers, magazine covers, album art. Styles (dark backgrounds, neo-minimalism, cinematic text) are delivery systems that change every six months. These elements are the engine underneath, and they do not change.

Every thumbnail must deliberately execute on **at least two** of these five. One is usually not enough to stop a scroll. More than three can create visual noise. Two or three, executed cleanly, is the target.

### 1. Curiosity

An information gap the brain is compelled to close. Something is implied but not explained. The viewer feels they will miss something if they do not click.

**How to engineer it:** Show a consequence without showing the cause. Show a reaction without showing what triggered it. Show a transformation without showing how it happened. The gap between what the viewer sees and what the viewer needs to know is the click.

**Finance/history examples:**
- A character staring at a document with visible shock — what does it say?
- A pile of coins visibly shrinking across a timeline — what is eating them?
- A character walking away from a building that is crumbling — what went wrong?

**Common mistake:** Showing the answer in the thumbnail. If the video is about how debt compounds, do not show a chart of compound interest — that closes the gap. Show the character buried under something growing, and let the viewer wonder what it is.

### 2. Contrast

A biological stop signal. In a feed of busy, cluttered, colorful thumbnails, the image that looks different interrupts the eye. This is neurological — the brain is hardwired to notice pattern breaks.

**How to engineer it:** Study what the feed looks like for this topic. If competing thumbnails are bright and busy, go dark and minimal. If they are all dark, use a single bold color. The goal is to be the thing that does not look like everything around it.

**For this channel:** The Boondocks animation style is already a strong contrast against the photo-real thumbnails dominating finance YouTube. The dark background (#0d1117) is clean against cluttered feeds. Lean into this — simplicity is the contrast strategy. A single character, a single object, a single piece of text. Silence in a room full of noise.

**Common mistake:** Adding visual elements to "make it more interesting." Every element you add makes the thumbnail look more like the feed, not less. Restraint is the contrast.

### 3. Emotion

A human face with a clear, readable emotion is processed by the brain in under 150 milliseconds — faster than the viewer can consciously read the title. The emotional response happens before the viewer knows what the video is about. That is the hook.

**How to engineer it:** The character's expression must be specific and readable at thumbnail size (small on a phone screen). Vague expressions do not register. The emotion should match the video's emotional peak — the single most intense moment — not the video's average tone.

**Emotion range for this channel:**

| Video Moment | Expression | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Discovering a hidden truth | Eyes widened, brow raised, mouth slightly open — genuine realization | The video reveals something most people do not know |
| Confronting an injustice | Jaw set, brow lowered, direct eye contact — quiet anger | The video exposes a system designed to exploit |
| Calculating a painful truth | Looking down at something in hands, brow furrowed — processing weight | The video shows math that changes how you see money |
| Warning the viewer | Leaning slightly forward, one hand raised palm-out, serious eyes — urgency | The video warns about a trap or mistake |
| Resigned understanding | Slight head tilt, knowing half-smile, tired eyes — "I've seen this before" | The video connects a historical pattern to the present |

The previous default — "knowing, grave, or quietly intense" — is one valid register but not the only one. Match the emotion to the video's most powerful moment, and make sure that emotion is legible at 120x68 pixels (the smallest thumbnail size on mobile).

### 4. Conflict

Something is unresolved. There is something at stake. Tension the viewer can feel that they are compelled to see resolved. Conflict implies a winner and a loser, a right and a wrong — and the viewer wants to know which side they are on.

**How to engineer it:** Show two forces in opposition. A character versus a system. A small thing versus a large thing. An old world versus a new world. The viewer should be able to feel that something is about to break, has broken, or is being contested.

**Finance/history examples:**
- Character standing small against a massive institutional building — individual vs. system
- A single coin on one side, a mountain of debt on the other — imbalance
- Character looking at two doors / two paths — forced choice
- Something valuable being taken or slipping away — loss in progress

**Common mistake:** Resolving the conflict in the thumbnail. If the video is about winning against a rigged system, do not show the character triumphant. Show the character facing the system. The resolution is the reward for clicking.

### 5. Reward

An implicit deal with the viewer: click this and you get something specific. Information, understanding, validation, an answer. The clearer the promised reward, the higher the CTR. Vague thumbnails get ignored. Specific promises get clicked.

**How to engineer it:** The thumbnail text carries most of the reward signal. "Hidden math" is vague. "$10/Day = $1.4M" is specific — the viewer knows exactly what they will learn. Pair specific text with a scene that makes the viewer need the explanation.

**Finance/history examples:**
- Text showing a specific number/equation + character reacting to it — "I need to understand this math"
- A before/after implied in a single frame — "I want to see how this transforms"
- A character examining something the viewer cannot quite see — "I want to see what he sees"

**Common mistake:** Making the reward too abstract. "The truth about money" promises nothing specific. "Why your savings lose 3% every year" promises a specific answer to a specific problem the viewer did not know they had.

## The Thumbnail Design Process

Follow this sequence for every thumbnail. Do not jump to scene descriptions until the concept is psychologically grounded.

### Step 1 — Identify the video's emotional peak

Read the full script. Find the single most intense, surprising, or consequential moment. This is not necessarily the hook — it is the moment where a viewer who watched the whole video would say "that part changed how I think." The thumbnail should promise this moment without delivering it.

### Step 2 — Choose your psychology

Pick 2-3 of the five elements that fit this video's peak moment. Write them down explicitly:

```
Elements: Curiosity (what is happening to the coins?) + Conflict (small vs. massive) + Reward (specific number revealed)
```

Every scene description and text choice that follows must serve at least one of these chosen elements. If a design choice does not serve any of them, cut it.

### Step 3 — Write the promise, not the description

Draft a one-sentence "thumbnail promise" — what the viewer will feel compelled to find out:

```
Promise: "Something is silently draining this man's money and he just realized what it is."
```

Test it: does this sentence make you want to know more? Or does it feel like a summary? If it is a summary, rewrite until it creates a gap.

### Step 4 — Select thumbnail text

Pull from the THUMBNAIL TEXT options in the script. Evaluate each option against:

1. **Information gap** — Does this text open a question or close one?
2. **Specificity** — Does it promise something concrete (a number, a mechanism, a reveal)?
3. **Brevity** — Can it be read in under 2 seconds at thumbnail size?
4. **Complement** — Does it add information the scene alone does not convey, or does it repeat what the image already shows?

The strongest text opens a gap that the image alone cannot close. The image shows something happening; the text hints at why or how much or what it means.

**Ranking method:** For each text option, score 0-2 on each of the four criteria above. Pick the top scorer for variation 1. Pick options that use different psychological elements for variations 2 and 3 — do not just pick the next-best scorer of the same type.

### Step 5 — Write the three scene descriptions

Each of the 3 variations should lean on a **different combination** of the five elements. If variation 1 is Curiosity + Conflict, variation 2 might be Emotion + Reward, and variation 3 might be Contrast + Curiosity. This gives the A/B test real diversity — three variations of the same psychology do not test anything useful.

For each scene, write the description following these rules:

**Character action must imply a story.** Not "standing in a vault" (static, descriptive) but "stepping back from an open vault, hand covering his mouth" (dynamic, implies something just happened). Every action should make the viewer ask "what happened?" or "what is about to happen?"

**Objects must create questions, not answers.** Not "a chart showing compound interest" (answer) but "a document with a number circled in red" (question — what is the number?). The viewer should see enough to be intrigued but not enough to be satisfied.

**Environment supports the conflict, not the topic.** The setting should amplify tension. A character in a bank is descriptive. A character dwarfed by a bank's massive marble columns is conflict (individual vs. institution). A character alone in an empty bank after hours is curiosity (why is he here?).

**Scale and composition create emotion.** A close-up of the character's face sells emotion. A wide shot with the character small against something large sells conflict. A medium shot with the character interacting with an object sells curiosity. Choose the composition that serves the element you are executing on.

### Step 6 — Run the composition checklist

Before finalizing the [THUMBNAIL] block, verify each variation:

| Check | What to verify |
|---|---|
| **Promise test** | Would a viewer feel compelled to click, or do they already get the point? |
| **Element count** | Does this variation execute on 2-3 of the five elements? Which ones? |
| **Element diversity** | Do the 3 variations cover different element combinations? |
| **Emotion legibility** | Is the character's expression readable at 120x68 pixels (phone size)? |
| **Text complement** | Does the text add information the image alone does not convey? |
| **Gap integrity** | Is the information gap open? Nothing in the image + text combination answers the question it raises? |
| **Contrast check** | In a feed of finance thumbnails, would this stop the scroll? |
| **Action, not pose** | Is the character doing something (mid-action) or just standing/sitting? |
| **Single focal point** | Is there one clear thing to look at first, or does the eye wander? |

If any variation fails two or more checks, redesign it before writing the [THUMBNAIL] block.

## Output Format

The skill's output integrates with the existing pipeline. After completing the design process, write the [THUMBNAIL] block in this format:

```markdown
## [THUMBNAIL]

title: [Top-scoring text from Step 4]
subtitle: [Secondary text or tagline — optional]

titles:
  - [Variation 1 text — different element emphasis]
  - [Variation 2 text — different element emphasis]
  - [Variation 3 text — different element emphasis]

subtitles:
  - [Variation 1 subtitle]
  - [Variation 2 subtitle]
  - [Variation 3 subtitle]

scenes:
  - [Variation 1: Scene description following Step 5 rules — character action, objects, environment, composition. Note which 2-3 elements this executes on.]
  - [Variation 2: Different element combination, different composition]
  - [Variation 3: Different element combination, different composition]
```

**Scene description requirements for generate_thumbnail.py compatibility:**
- Must describe character appearance, clothing, action, and surroundings in a single block of text
- Clothing must be period/topic-appropriate (the script reads this for character dress)
- Expression must be specific and match the chosen emotion from the range table
- Do not include photography terms (photorealistic, macro photography, etc.) — the Boondocks animation style is enforced by the generation script
- Keep each scene under 200 words — the prompt has a lot of surrounding context and overly long scenes can dilute the instruction

## Quick Reference — What Each Element Looks Like in a Thumbnail

| Element | Visual Signal | Text Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Curiosity** | Partially hidden object, character reacting to something off-screen, incomplete action | Question, ellipsis, "the X nobody talks about" |
| **Contrast** | Clean/minimal composition, unusual color, pattern break from feed norms | Short vs. long, small number vs. big number |
| **Emotion** | Close-up face, specific readable expression, eye contact or intense gaze | Emotional language — "trap", "cost", "lost" |
| **Conflict** | Size disparity, opposing forces, character vs. environment, tension in body language | "vs.", imbalance, "the real reason" |
| **Reward** | Specific object/number visible, implied transformation, "answer" partially shown | Exact number, equation, specific promise |

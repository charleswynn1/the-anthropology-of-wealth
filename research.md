# Research Prompt Template

Use this prompt when conducting research for a new episode topic. Replace `[INSERT TOPIC]` with the actual topic before running.

---

You are an elite research analyst, documentary development strategist, and long-form YouTube story researcher.

Your job is to conduct an EXHAUSTIVE research investigation on the following topic for my current project:

TOPIC: [INSERT TOPIC]
PROJECT: YouTube channel about the hidden history of money, credit, debt, banking, trade, currency, financial systems, and the things ordinary people do not know.
GOAL: Produce a deep research dossier that helps me create a highly engaging, historically grounded, story-driven YouTube video.
AUDIENCE: Curious everyday adults who are interested in money, history, hidden systems, power, and how the past still affects modern life.
TONE: Intelligent, clear, vivid, human, story-rich, non-academic, compelling, and highly usable for scriptwriting.
PRIORITY: Depth, exhaustiveness, nuance, historical accuracy, hidden details, uncommon insights, tensions, contradictions, and strong story material.

IMPORTANT INSTRUCTIONS:
1. Be exhaustive rather than brief.
2. Do not give me a shallow summary.
3. Do not just give obvious facts. I want known facts, lesser-known facts, hidden angles, tensions, and surprising implications.
4. Organize the output clearly with strong headings and subheadings.
5. Where claims may be disputed, note the uncertainty.
6. Distinguish between confirmed historical consensus, debated interpretations, myths, and popular simplifications.
7. Prioritize material that would be useful for:
   - storytelling
   - hooks
   - audience curiosity
   - emotional tension
   - modern relevance
   - visual scenes
   - scriptwriting
8. When possible, identify the "why this matters" for ordinary people today.
9. Include both macro history and concrete human examples.
10. Avoid fluff, generic motivation, or vague filler.
11. Assume I may turn this into a long-form YouTube video, a short-form clip, a thread, a podcast monologue, or a documentary outline.

DELIVER THE RESEARCH IN THE FOLLOWING STRUCTURE:

SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE OVERVIEW
- Explain the topic in plain English.
- Why this topic matters in the history of money.
- Why it is important, strange, controversial, or misunderstood.
- What most people think about this topic.
- What most people do NOT realize about this topic.
- The single most important insight.
- The most surprising insight.
- The most emotionally compelling angle.
- The best "this changed everything" angle.

SECTION 2: CORE DEFINITION AND BOUNDARIES
- Define the topic clearly.
- Define related terms people confuse with it.
- Explain what this topic includes and does not include.
- Clarify different schools of thought or historical interpretations.
- Give the simplest usable explanation and the most precise explanation.

SECTION 3: ORIGIN STORY
- Where did this begin?
- What conditions made it necessary or possible?
- What problem was it solving at the time?
- What came before it?
- Who were the earliest known participants, inventors, users, institutions, or societies involved?
- What is the best evidence for its origin?
- What parts of the origin story are unclear or debated?

SECTION 4: HISTORICAL TIMELINE
Create a chronological timeline with major phases, turning points, and transformations.
For each phase include:
- approximate dates
- what changed
- why it changed
- who drove the change
- who benefited
- who was harmed
- what new problems were created
- how this phase influenced later money systems

SECTION 5: KEY PEOPLE, GROUPS, AND INSTITUTIONS
Identify the most important:
- individuals
- rulers
- merchants
- bankers
- governments
- empires
- religious institutions
- classes of people
- trade networks
- private firms
- central authorities
For each, explain:
- their role
- their incentives
- what they gained
- what they changed
- how history judges them
- any interesting contradiction or irony

SECTION 6: ECONOMIC AND SOCIAL MECHANICS
Explain how this topic actually worked in practice.
Break down:
- the mechanism
- the incentives
- who trusted it
- why people accepted it
- what made it fragile
- what made it powerful
- how it spread
- how it became normalized
- how it changed everyday life
- how it altered trade, class, labor, power, family life, law, religion, or government

SECTION 7: HIDDEN SYSTEMS AND POWER DYNAMICS
Analyze the hidden structures beneath the topic:
- power
- control
- dependency
- legitimacy
- coercion
- surveillance
- gatekeeping
- scarcity
- status
- state authority
- elite benefit
- mass behavior
- incentives that were invisible to ordinary people
Explain:
- who really controlled the system
- who appeared to control it
- how trust was manufactured or maintained
- how the system shaped behavior
- how the system could be abused
- how this topic relates to broader social order

SECTION 8: RELIGION, MORALITY, AND CULTURE
Research moral and cultural dimensions:
- was it seen as good, evil, necessary, sinful, modern, dangerous, corrupting, liberating, or prestigious?
- what did religious traditions say about it?
- what did ordinary people believe about it?
- what taboos or moral objections existed?
- how did culture normalize or resist it?
- what stories, sayings, proverbs, teachings, or myths formed around it?

SECTION 9: CONFLICT, CONTROVERSY, AND ABUSE
Identify:
- scandals
- fraud
- manipulation
- exploitation
- political fights
- class tensions
- debt traps
- legal battles
- moral panics
- collapses
- reforms
- unintended consequences
Explain:
- what happened
- why it happened
- who paid the price
- what changed afterward
- what patterns repeated over time

SECTION 10: WHAT PEOPLE GET WRONG
List the biggest myths, simplifications, and misunderstandings.
For each:
- state the myth
- explain why people believe it
- explain what is more accurate
- note whether the truth is debated or well-established

SECTION 11: MODERN LEGACY
Explain how this topic still affects life today.
Connect it to:
- wages
- inflation
- banking
- loans
- credit
- state power
- markets
- real estate
- taxes
- inequality
- consumer behavior
- digital payments
- public trust
- financial crises
- modern political debates
Show the exact line from past to present.

SECTION 12: BEST STORY ANGLES FOR YOUTUBE
Generate at least 15 strong angles for a YouTube video on this topic.
For each angle include:
- title premise
- curiosity hook
- emotional hook
- modern relevance
- who the villain/problem is
- who the victim/stakes are
- what the audience learns

SECTION 13: BEST HOOKS
Generate:
- 20 opening hook lines
- 10 "what if everything you know is wrong" hooks
- 10 "the real story is darker/stranger than you think" hooks
- 10 simple, plainspoken hooks for mainstream viewers

SECTION 14: SCRIPTING GOLD
Pull out the best material for storytelling:
- the most cinematic moments
- the most dramatic turning points
- the most surprising facts
- the strongest human stories
- the clearest symbolic moments
- the most visually rich details
- the sharpest irony
- the most quotable takeaways
- the best one-sentence insights
- the best "this is where everything changed" moments

SECTION 15: VISUAL RESEARCH
Suggest visuals that would strengthen a documentary-style video:
- maps
- artifacts
- coins
- paper notes
- ledgers
- contracts
- paintings
- statues
- engravings
- trade routes
- charts
- recreations
- locations
- social scenes
- institutional imagery
For each, explain what story point the visual helps communicate.

SECTION 16: COMPARISONS AND CONTRASTS
Compare this topic with:
- what existed before it
- what replaced it
- similar systems in other societies
- modern versions of the same idea
- common alternatives people wrongly assume were better or simpler
Highlight meaningful contrasts, not just surface differences.

SECTION 17: FIRST-ORDER, SECOND-ORDER, AND THIRD-ORDER EFFECTS
Break the impact into layers:
- First-order: immediate effects
- Second-order: social and economic consequences
- Third-order: civilizational consequences over time
Then explain which consequences still shape society now.

SECTION 18: EVERYDAY LIFE IMPACT
Describe how this topic changed ordinary life for:
- workers
- families
- women
- merchants
- peasants
- debtors
- rulers
- soldiers
- consumers
- migrants
- religious communities
- the poor
- the wealthy
Make this concrete, not abstract.

SECTION 19: RESEARCH GAPS AND OPEN QUESTIONS
Identify:
- what historians still debate
- weak evidence areas
- unresolved questions
- contested narratives
- where propaganda or ideology shaped the record
- where modern retellings oversimplify the past

SECTION 20: SOURCE MAP
Provide a categorized source map, not just a random list.
Group into:
- primary sources
- scholarly books
- academic papers
- museum resources
- reputable history institutions
- economics history sources
- articles for general audiences
- archives and image sources
For each source, explain what it is best for.

SECTION 21: CONTENT EXTRACTION
Now extract the research into usable creator assets:
A. 25 video titles
B. 25 thumbnail text ideas
C. 10 shorts ideas
D. 10 podcast episode angles
E. 10 thread/post angles
F. 5 series ideas this topic could belong to
G. 5 contrarian takes grounded in history
H. 5 "most people don't know" insights
I. 5 emotional story frames
J. 5 educational-but-clickable framing devices

SECTION 22: FINAL SYNTHESIS
End with:
- the deepest truth about this topic
- the best overall framing for a YouTube episode
- the strongest narrative arc
- the best audience takeaway
- the single sentence that best captures why this matters

OUTPUT REQUIREMENTS:
- Be detailed.
- Be exhaustive.
- Use headings, subheadings, and structured formatting.
- Do not collapse nuance.
- Do not skip controversial or uncomfortable aspects.
- Do not overuse jargon.
- Make the research directly usable for content creation.
- Write with clarity and density.
- Favor insight over filler.
- Favor specifics over generalities.
- Favor story value over textbook tone.

Before finishing, include a final section called:
"WHAT I WOULD EMPHASIZE IF THIS WERE MY VIDEO"
In that section, tell me:
- what angle you would choose
- what hook you would open with
- what emotional tone you would use
- what 3 to 5 story beats you would structure the video around
- what lesson would leave the strongest impression on viewers

## PHASE 2: DEEP EXPANSION PROMPT

After completing Phase 1 and saving the output, run this second pass against the same topic. Use the Phase 1 output as your context. Do not repeat the same wording unless necessary. The goal is to surface what Phase 1 missed or underserved.

---

Good. Now go deeper.

I want you to expand the weakest sections, add more hidden angles, add more tensions and contradictions, and strengthen the parts most useful for storytelling and scriptwriting.

TOPIC: [INSERT TOPIC]

You have already completed a full Phase 1 research pass on this topic. Now go back in and improve it. Use the following as your directive:

SPECIFICALLY IMPROVE THESE 8 AREAS:

**1. LESSER-KNOWN FACTS**
Go beyond the obvious. Find:
- facts that trained historians know but general audiences rarely encounter
- counterintuitive findings that contradict common assumptions
- details that were suppressed, overlooked, or lost in mainstream retellings
- surprising statistics, measurements, or records from primary sources
- specific people, dates, places, or events that add texture and credibility
- anything that would make a curious person say "I had no idea"

**2. HISTORICAL DEBATES**
Surface the real fights:
- where historians, economists, or scholars actively disagree
- which interpretations are politically or ideologically motivated
- which "facts" are actually contested or unverifiable
- where the popular version of history was shaped by propaganda, nationalism, or winner bias
- what the minority scholarly view is and why it has merit
- where new research has challenged old consensus
- what we genuinely do not know and why

**3. HUMAN STORIES**
Find the people:
- specific named individuals whose lives illustrate the topic
- ordinary people (not just elites) who were affected in documented ways
- real documented cases of exploitation, resistance, ingenuity, or survival
- stories with clear protagonists, antagonists, stakes, and outcomes
- moments where one person's decision changed a system
- stories that carry moral weight or emotional resonance
- stories that could anchor a scene or segment in a video

**4. POWER DYNAMICS**
Go beneath the surface:
- who held real power versus ceremonial or apparent power
- how power was transferred, captured, or consolidated around this topic
- what leverage points existed and who controlled them
- how ordinary people were made dependent without realizing it
- where coercion was disguised as consent or normality
- how the powerful protected the system from reform
- what happened to people who tried to resist or exit the system
- how this power dynamic evolved over time

**5. MODERN RELEVANCE**
Connect past to present with specificity:
- exact modern institutions, laws, policies, or systems descended from this topic
- how this topic explains something people experience today but cannot name
- specific current behaviors, assumptions, or norms that trace back here
- economic mechanisms today that work the same way
- industries or institutions that profit from the legacy of this topic
- where the same pattern is playing out right now under a different name
- what understanding this topic changes about how you see the modern world

**6. VISUAL DETAILS**
Make it cinematic:
- specific physical objects, documents, buildings, or artifacts that carry the story
- what people were wearing, carrying, building, trading, or using
- what the environments looked and smelled and felt like
- what the key moments of tension looked like in real life
- specific images, paintings, maps, or engravings that depict this topic
- what a scene-by-scene visual recreation of a key moment would look like
- what everyday life looked like for people embedded in this system

**7. EMOTIONALLY COMPELLING MOMENTS**
Find the scenes that hit:
- moments of injustice that would anger a modern viewer
- moments of ingenuity or courage that would inspire
- moments of irony so sharp they feel almost comedic
- moments of tragedy caused by ignorance or by design
- the moment a person realized they had been deceived or trapped
- the moment a system crossed a line and people pushed back
- the moment everything changed and nothing was ever the same
- the human cost expressed in individual lives, not just statistics

**8. YOUTUBE HOOKS AND TITLE ANGLES**
Generate more and better:
- 15 additional opening hook lines not in the Phase 1 output
- 10 additional video title options with different angles and tones
- 5 angles specifically designed for an audience aware of systemic financial issues
- 5 angles that use irony, reversal, or "the thing you think you know is backwards"
- 5 angles built around a specific person, moment, or event from the research
- 3 "conspiracy-adjacent but historically accurate" angles that feel urgent
- the single strongest hook you would bet money on

ADDITIONAL INSTRUCTIONS:
- Search for additional sources not covered in Phase 1.
- Prioritize primary sources, specialist historians, and non-mainstream scholarship.
- If you find something that contradicts Phase 1, note the contradiction and explain which version has stronger evidence.
- Add any important topic areas that Phase 1 missed entirely.
- Do not pad with repetition. Every paragraph must earn its place.
- Write with the same clarity, density, and story-first orientation as Phase 1.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Structure your Phase 2 output with the same headings used in Phase 1 where relevant.
Label each section clearly as "PHASE 2 EXPANSION: [Section Name]" so it can be merged cleanly.
End with an updated "WHAT I WOULD EMPHASIZE IF THIS WERE MY VIDEO" that supersedes the Phase 1 version.

Merge Phase 2 findings into `projects/<project>/topic_research.md`. Do not create a separate file. Integrate the expansions under their corresponding sections and replace the "WHAT I WOULD EMPHASIZE" section with the updated version.

## PHASE 3: ANGLE SELECTION PROMPT

After completing Phase 2 and merging all research, run this final pass. Use the full combined research as your context. This phase does not add new facts — it makes a strategic editorial decision.

---

Based on this research, identify the single strongest video angle for a mainstream YouTube audience and explain why it is stronger than the others.

TOPIC: [INSERT TOPIC]

You have now completed two full research passes on this topic. You have a deep dossier covering history, mechanics, power dynamics, human stories, modern relevance, hooks, and title angles.

Now make the call.

YOUR TASK:

Review everything in the research and answer the following with precision and conviction. Do not hedge. Do not list every option equally. Pick one angle and defend it.

**1. THE WINNING ANGLE**
State the single strongest video angle in one sentence.
- What is the central claim or premise of this angle?
- What question does it answer that the audience is already asking (even if they don't know it)?
- What makes this angle different from a standard explainer on this topic?

**2. WHY THIS ANGLE WINS**
Explain why this angle is stronger than the others. Be specific.
- What makes it more clickable than the runner-up angles?
- What makes it more emotionally resonant?
- What makes it more relevant to a mainstream audience today?
- What makes it easier to tell as a story (clear protagonist, clear stakes, clear lesson)?
- What makes it more visually rich or cinematically compelling?
- What makes it more likely to be shared or recommended?

**3. THE RUNNER-UP ANGLES**
Name the 2 to 3 strongest competing angles and explain exactly why they lose to the winner.
- Where does each runner-up fall short (too niche, too abstract, too slow to hook, not emotionally grounded, etc.)?
- Could any runner-up work as a follow-up video or a secondary layer inside the winning angle?

**4. THE HOOK**
Write the single best opening line for this angle. One sentence. It must:
- create immediate curiosity or tension
- feel spoken, not written
- not require prior knowledge to understand
- make a viewer stop scrolling

**5. THE NARRATIVE ARC**
Map the video using this angle into 4 to 6 story beats. For each beat:
- what happens in this beat
- what the viewer feels or learns
- what visual or scene carries the beat
- what question it answers or opens

**6. THE CORE LESSON**
What is the single sentence takeaway the viewer carries out of this video?
It must be:
- memorable enough to repeat to someone else
- specific enough to be actionable or mind-shifting
- emotionally satisfying as a conclusion

**7. THUMBNAIL AND TITLE**
Based on this winning angle:
- Write the single best video title
- Write the single best thumbnail text (2 to 3 words, hook-style)
- Explain why these outperform the other options generated in Phase 1 and Phase 2

**8. RISK ASSESSMENT**
What could go wrong with this angle?
- Is any part of it potentially misleading if oversimplified?
- Is there a nuance that must be preserved to keep the video honest?
- Is there a framing trap to avoid (clickbait that overpromises, moral conclusions the evidence does not support)?
- How do you execute this angle without losing accuracy or integrity?

OUTPUT FORMAT:
Write the Phase 3 output as a clean, standalone editorial brief — not as raw research notes.
It should read like a confident creative director giving a final recommendation.
Append it to `projects/<project>/topic_research.md` under a clearly labeled section:

## PHASE 3: ANGLE SELECTION

Do not modify any earlier sections. Add Phase 3 as a new final section at the bottom of the file.
